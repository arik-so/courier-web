Cross plattform

Courier is cross-platform-compatible and supports unlimited simultaneous login sessions, thus allowing for simultaneous data access from smartphones, tablets, laptops, and desktops. The transmitted data cannot be decrypted by any external entities; only the senders and recipients can access it.

Courier technology

  • 4096-bit asymmetric RSA keypairs
  • 256-bit symmetric AES keys with 128-bit IVs employing the CBC algorithm
  • All encryption and decryption operations are performed client-side only
  • Data processing conformant to German "BDSG" standards

FAQs

What happens when I create a new user account?

When you create a new account, you generate a random asymmetric 4096-bit RSA keypair. Your public key is sent to the server as is, whereas your private key is symmetrically encrypted using a deterministic algorithm than can only be executed on your client device and is impossible to replicate anywhere else.

Simultaneously, your password is deterministically salted and hashed, and then sent to the server. The server in turn hashes your password again, this time with a random salt, and stores the result and the other data you sent.

What happens when I log in?

When you log in, you send your username and the deterministically salted and hashed password to the server. The server checks it against its records, and if it determines a match, your public key and your encrypted private key are sent back to you. Using the other deterministic algorithm than can only be executed on your device, your private key is decrypted and made temporarily accessible on your device for the duration of the session.

Can I reset my password in case I forget it?

No, you can't. Your password is not stored anywhere, yet it is a factor in the deterministic algorithm for the encryption of your private key. If you lose your password, you lose all your data, as it becomes cryptographically impossible to decrypt without it.

We, as the service provider, only have access to the encrypted data, and are never ever able to decrypt it. Therefore, all you can do in the case that you lose or forget your password is create a new account.

How are my login data stored locally?

By default, your login data are only ever stored in the RAM, so when you log out or quit the application, they are permanently removed from all storage. If you choose to stay logged in, however, your password is stored using Apple's keychain mechanism with the highest security option possible, i. e., the data are not synced using iCloud Keychain and are only accessible while your device is unlocked.

If you are using a device with Touch ID, we especially recommend to refrain from using 4-digit-passcodes on your device, but use longer and sophisticated passwords instead.

What happens when I create a new conversation?

When you create a new conversation, you first define the other members of that conversation and fetch their public RSA keys. Then, a random 256-bit AES key is generated, and encrypted with each of the correspondents' public keys. These asymmetrically encrypted variants of the AES key are sent to their respective recipients. Additionally, the AES key is signed with your private key and that signature is stored centrally on the server for the conversation you just created.

When your future correspondents receive their respective encrypted AES keys, they use their private keys to decrypt them in order to obtain the conversation's actual AES key, which they can later use to decrypt the messages.

What happens when I send a message?

A random 128-bit initialization vector is generated, which is used to encrypt the message with the conversation's 256-bit AES key using the CBC algorithm. Additionally, the message is signed with your private key. The initialization vector, the encrypted message, and its signature are sent to the server.

How are my messages relayed to my correspondents?

When your message arrives at the server, the other correspondents are notified of its arrival. The encrypted message, its signature, and the initialization vector are sent to them. They then use the initialization vector and the conversation's AES key to decrypt your message, and verify its authenticity using the signature.

How do I verify my correspondents' identities?

In order to verify that the public RSA keys you use to encrypt the conversations' AES keys truly belong to your contacts and are not the result of a man-in-the-middle attack, a pair of secrets and their respective signatures is randomly generated whenever you send or confirm a contact request.

To check the public key authenticity, just go to the contact's detail page, click on exchange secrets, and then either scan each other's QR codes in person, or send your secrets using any external medium, preferably one where you can hear each other's voices.

Due to the fact that the secrets are different for each pair of contacts, we are the only messenger which, on top of actually allowing to double-check your contacts' public keys, also makes sure that the possibility of a web of trust is eliminated.

How can you synchronize my address book without obtaining my contacts' information?

We anonymize your contact data before sending it to our servers. That means, we iterate through all of your contacts, get their emails and phone numbers, and deterministically hash them (without salting because it's not possible in this scenario) and then send the results to the server. Your own contact details are marked as belonging to your user, whereas the others are simply linked.

When a different user then synchronizes their address book, and we notice that one of their contacts' details matches some details that are marked as belonging to you (thus ensuring mutual acquaintance and consent to being linked on Instari), both of you automatically send or confirm each other contact requests. However, that does not include identity verification, because only you, a real person and user, can do that step.

Following up on the previous question, how can you make sure I own my email address without exposing my identity to you and your servers?

We have developed a sophisticated method for anonymized identity confirmation (quite the oxymoron, right?). Before you can import your address book, you first have to enter your email address. A request is sent to the server with that email address but no token data or user IDs, and the server returns a deterministically salted hash of the 6-digit-code, while sending an email to the given address where the code is in plain text.

Back on your device, you have to enter the 6-digit-code you received via email, and the device then compares the hashes of the codes. Upon encountering a match, you can import your address book, and the application detects the email in your address book and marks it as yours.
